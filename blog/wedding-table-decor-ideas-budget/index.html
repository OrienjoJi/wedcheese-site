Wedding Table Decor Ideas by Budget

Updated June 2026~8 min read

Table decor sets the mood for hours of conversation, photos, and cake-cutting—but it is also where couples overspend on items guests barely notice. The trick is matching wedding table decor ideas to a clear budget tier, then tracking costs before you order twelve identical centrepieces.

Start with your table count and shape: long banqueting tables favour runners and repeated bud vases; round tables suit centrepieces with 360-degree views. Your venue may supply basics—chargers, chairs, standard linen—that change what you actually need to buy or hire.

Low budget: focus on light, linen, and one hero per table

Under roughly £25–£40 per table, prioritise what photographs and what guests touch. Crisp linen or a runner in your wedding colour palette lifts a plain table instantly. Add one simple centrepiece: bud vases with seasonal flowers, a pillar candle cluster on a tray, or a bowl of foraged greenery (with venue fire rules in mind).

Skip expensive chair covers unless the venue chairs genuinely clash. Borrow glassware or candle holders from family, and reuse ceremony flowers at the top table where guests will notice most. Our DIY decor guide explains what saves money versus what costs stress.

Mid budget: layers, texture, and cohesive place settings

At roughly £40–£90 per table, you can layer texture without custom floristry on every surface. Mix heights: low greenery ring plus two taller candles. Add charger plates or napkins with a simple fold, place cards, and a modest favour if it fits your style.

Rent rather than buy where possible—candelabras, gold cutlery, and glass domes add polish for one evening’s hire fee. Tie each table back to two or three palette colours so the room feels designed, not assembled from random Pinterest saves.

Higher budget: statement florals and curated details

Above roughly £90 per table, you are paying for floristry labour, rare blooms, and structural pieces—hanging installations, elevated centrepieces, or custom linens. This tier works when photography and room scale matter: high ceilings, long banqueting tables, or a blank canvas venue that needs drama.

Even here, allocate deliberately. One lavish top table and simpler guest tables often photographs better than identical luxury everywhere—and keeps the hidden costs of delivery, setup, and breakdown visible in your budget.

Build a table decor checklist

Walk the room table by table: linen, centrepiece, candles, place settings, numbers, favours, and any venue restrictions (open flames, confetti, sticking pins into hired furniture). A wedding decor checklist by area stops you ordering signage twice while forgetting napkin rings.

Log each line item with quantity (number of tables × settings per table) and assign a budget category. When florists quote “per table,” confirm whether that includes setup, vases, and collection—or only stems.

Beautiful tables do not require maximum spend—they require one coherent idea repeated calmly across the room. Choose your tier, checklist the pieces, and track the maths before you sign the florist proposal.

Photography and guest experience

Tables matter in pictures long after guests leave. Low centrepieces keep faces visible across the table; tall arrangements look dramatic in room shots but can block conversation. Discuss sight lines with your photographer before you commit to towering florals on every round table.

Candles photograph beautifully but trigger venue fire rules—LED alternatives exist at every budget tier now. Test one table setup during a planning visit if your venue allows it; lighting at 7pm looks nothing like a daytime tour.

When to splurge versus spread spend

Concentrate budget on the top table, cake table, and entrance path if you need impact on a mid-tier total. Guests remember warmth, pacing, and food more than whether every napkin had a bespoke wax seal. Track each decor line in WedCheese so you can see trade-offs clearly—swap chair bows for better linen before you overspend on favours nobody keeps.

Rentals versus purchases

Low-budget couples buy what guests take home (candles, small vases) and rent what you would never store (chargers, arches, lounge furniture). Mid-tier couples rent linens and tableware even when buying florals—laundering fifty napkins is not a fun post-wedding task. Higher-tier setups often rent everything including custom signage, with strike handled overnight.

Ask hire companies about package minimums and delivery windows; late collection fees hide in hidden wedding costs lists for a reason.

Whatever tier you choose, photograph one finished table during setup—your future self will thank you when reordering candles or confirming linen shades.
