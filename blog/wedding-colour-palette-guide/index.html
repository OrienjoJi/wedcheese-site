How to Choose a Wedding Colour Palette

Updated June 2026~8 min read

A wedding colour palette is the thread that ties florals, stationery, bridesmaid dresses, table linen, and cake icing into something that looks intentional—not assembled from separate Pinterest saves. Choose badly and every supplier interprets “blush and gold” differently; choose well and even budget hire looks cohesive.

This guide explains how to pick colours that work in British light (grey skies included), how many tones to use, and how WedCheese helps with nineteen curated palettes plus AI styling tied to your decor checklist.

How many colours—and which roles

Most successful palettes use three to five colours with clear jobs: one dominant (often neutral or soft tone), one accent for flowers and ties, and one metallic or deep contrast for lettering and candles. Avoid giving every bridesmaid a different shade unless you want a rainbow group photo that fights the venue walls.

Test swatches against your venue in person. Burgundy that glows in a candlelit barn can look muddy under fluorescent hotel ceiling tiles. Photograph fabric samples next to wood, stone, or carpet you cannot change.

Consider season and time of day: summer garden weddings tolerate brighter pops; winter evening receptions often suit richer, deeper bases with warm lighting.

Where your palette must appear

List every touchpoint before you commit. Florals, stationery, ribbon, cake, signage, lounge cushions, and even the wedding website should pull from the same core set. Suppliers appreciate hex codes or Pantone references—not “something sage-ish.”

Do not forget practical items: guest book pens, emergency sewing kits in favour bags, and wrap on your getting-ready robes. Small mismatches stand out in flat-lay photos.

Common palette mistakes to avoid

Matching bridesmaid fabric to a Pantone swatch on screen is unreliable—order samples. Avoid too many metallics; rose gold plus copper plus gold reads chaotic in flash photography. If your venue has strong permanent colour (red carpet, green wallpaper), work with it rather than fighting it with clashing accents.

Remember skin tones and photography: very cool grey palettes can wash out complexions under overcast skies unless your photographer adjusts white balance. Warm candlelight flatters most evening palettes; test one rehearsal dinner look if you are unsure.

Stationery proof on the paper stock you chose, not on screen. Ivory ink on cream card disappears; dark navy on black looks sophisticated in PDF and unreadable in hand.

Share a one-page palette brief

Export your chosen colours as a simple brief for vendors: dominant, accent, neutral, metal finish, and “please avoid.” Include venue photos showing wall and floor tones. Florists, cake designers, and stationers produce better proofs when they are not guessing from a phone screenshot.

Revisit the palette after dress shopping—if your gown reads ivory and your stationery was planned in stark white, adjust early. Colour harmony is cheaper to fix on screen than on the day with mismatched napkins.
