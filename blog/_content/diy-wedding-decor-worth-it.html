Pinterest makes DIY wedding decor look effortless: hand-poured candles, calligraphy place cards, a floral arch assembled at midnight. Reality involves glue guns, borrowed car boots, and a hen party that turns into a production line. DIY can save money—or cost you sleep. The question is not “can we make it?” but “should we?”

This guide splits decor into what is worth doing yourself and what is better hired, with a nod to tracking costs in your wedding decor checklist so enthusiasm does not blow the budget.

The real maths: savings vs stress

DIY saves when materials are cheap, labour is free (friends and family), and mistakes are low-stakes. It costs when you need specialist tools, repeat attempts, or weekend after weekend of prep while other planning stalls.

Assign a “stress tax” in your head: if a project saves £200 but needs 15 hours and causes arguments, that is not a bargain. Compare against quotes in your vendor quote comparison workflow before committing.

Usually worth DIY

  • Signage and menus. Printed boards, framed quotes, and table menus are forgiving first projects.
  • Table numbers and place cards. Card stock, a good pen, or a simple template—done in an evening.
  • Candle clusters and votives. Bulk-buy plain holders; unify with one ribbon or wax colour.
  • Welcome display and guest book table. A styled console with photos and a pen beats a bespoke prop.
  • Favours you enjoy making. Jam, seed packets, or baked goods—if it is fun, not frantic.
  • Getting-ready room details. Hangers, mirrors, and robe hooks—small touches, big photos.

Usually worth hiring

  • Large floral installations. Arches, ceiling hangs, and aisle arrangements need skill and cold storage.
  • Linens and chair covers at scale. Steaming hundreds of covers the morning of is not a job for bridesmaids.
  • Lighting rigs. Uplighters and festoon wiring belong to electricians, not YouTube tutorials.
  • Anything suspended above guests. Safety and insurance matter; venues often require certified riggers.
  • Cake and catering presentation. Professionals deliver consistency when guests (and cameras) are watching.

The grey area: decide with three questions

Ask yourself: Can we test it once? If the first attempt looks amateur, you will either redo it or panic-buy replacements. Who sets it up on the day? You should not be stapling fabric at 7 a.m. What happens if it fails? A wonky sign is fine; a collapsed arch is not.

Centrepieces often sit here. Simple bud vases and greenery you assemble the night before can work. Tall, structural arrangements that block conversation across the table are florist territory. Our table decor ideas by budget post breaks down low, mid, and higher spend options.

Plan DIY decor like a project, not a hobby

List every item, quantity, supplier, and delivery date in one place—not scattered notes. Tie costs to your wedding budget so a trolley full of craft supplies does not silently eat the photography fund. Watch for hidden wedding costs like delivery fees, extra paint, and last-minute Amazon panic buys.

Choose a colour palette early and buy materials in that range. Mismatched “close enough” blush tones read as accidental in photos, not eclectic.

Delegate and deadline

Assign one trusted person as “decor lead” for the wedding week. They confirm what arrives when, who collects hired items, and what goes in which box. Build setup into your day-of timeline—not as an afterthought between hair and photos.

If DIY was meant to save money but you are now buying a second glue gun and paying rush delivery, stop and re-quote a florist or stylist. Sometimes the professional option is cheaper once you count your time.

When DIY is worth it emotionally

Not every DIY project is about pounds saved. A table your grandfather built, programs your sister lettered, or favours from a family recipe add meaning no hire company can replicate. Keep those pieces. Outsource the rest so you can enjoy them instead of assembling them in your pyjamas at 1 a.m.

Sample DIY budget sanity check

Before you buy, list: materials, tools you must purchase, labour hours, and a “panic line item” for replacements. If a centrepiece costs £8 per table in supplies but needs four hours for twenty tables, that is eighty hours—ask whether a florist quote of £600 all-in is actually better value. WedCheese lets you log decor line items beside hired quotes so the comparison stays honest, not optimistic.

Revisit the split monthly. Projects that felt fun at nine months out often feel like chores at six weeks. Give yourself permission to cancel a DIY plan and reallocate the budget—your sanity is a line item too.

Photographers notice when decor steals your morning. If setup runs long, you lose calm prep time and golden-hour flexibility. Hire the heavy lifting; keep the personal touches that fit in a single box.
