“Can we just use ChatGPT to plan our wedding?” It is the most common question couples ask when they already pay for a general AI subscription. The honest answer: ChatGPT is brilliant at writing—speeches, polite emails, theme brainstorming—but it is not an AI wedding planner in the sense that matters when deposits, RSVPs, and a fixed date are on the line.

Generic AI forgets your context between chats. It cannot see that you are over budget on florals, that fourteen guests have not replied, or that your ceremony starts at 2pm and your photographer only booked until 10pm. A dedicated planner closes that gap. This guide compares both approaches so you can use each where it actually helps.

What ChatGPT does well for weddings

Treat ChatGPT like a clever writing assistant—not your project manager. It shines at:

  • Drafting maid-of-honour speech outlines and thank-you note wording.
  • Brainstorming themes, hashtags, and colour palette directions.
  • Explaining etiquette questions in plain language.
  • Rewriting awkward emails to vendors or family when you paste in the details.
  • Suggesting wording for tricky situations—adults-only invites, no plus-ones, RSVP reminders.

For one-off creative tasks, it is often faster than opening a blank document. Our awkward wedding emails guide shows the kind of prompts that work well in generic AI—then explains when to move the result into a proper planning app.

Where generic AI falls short

Wedding planning is ongoing state management, not a series of isolated questions. ChatGPT struggles because:

  • No live wedding profile. You re-explain budget, date, and guest count every session.
  • No actions. It suggests “book a florist” but cannot search vendors, save quotes, or add a timeline task.
  • No schedule builder. It can describe a run sheet; it cannot generate one from your actual events and travel times.
  • No safety rails. It might propose a lavish upgrade when you said £15,000 three messages ago.
  • Copy-paste fatigue. Every RSVP shift means exporting from your spreadsheet back into the chat.
  • No guest-facing tools. It cannot issue RSVP links or track dietary requirements per guest.

You end up maintaining the real plan elsewhere—usually a spreadsheet, three notes apps, and a folder of PDF contracts. The AI becomes a side dish, not the kitchen.

What a dedicated AI wedding planner should do instead

The bar for a real AI wedding planner app is higher than fluent text. It should behave like an assistant with access to your data: read context, propose changes, and execute approved updates inside the app.

  • Know your current budget, categories, and headcount without being reminded.
  • Generate and adjust checklist tasks based on your wedding date—including accelerated timelines for short engagements.
  • Search vendors near your venue and log results beside your budget.
  • Issue personal RSVP links so guests reply without creating an account.
  • Draft proposals you review before anything is written to your profile.
  • Store notes, contracts, and decor ideas in a binder linked to vendors and tasks.

Crucially, a good app does not claim to replace human planners for on-the-day coordination or emotionally charged family negotiations. It handles admin so you have energy for the decisions only you can make.

What about human wedding planners?

Neither ChatGPT nor a planning app replaces an experienced coordinator who knows your venue’s loading bay, manages family dynamics, or runs cue sheets on the day. Many couples hire a human planner for month-of or day-of support while using an app for the twelve months of admin beforehand. AI handles spreadsheets, reminders, and draft emails; humans handle judgment calls and being physically present. That combination is often the sweet spot—not “AI instead of people,” but “AI so you have energy left for people.”

The smartest couples do not pick a winner—they pick the right tool for each job. Let generic AI handle words; let a dedicated planner handle the wedding.
