They cannot nudge you when a milestone slips.

Before you start: pick one source of truth

The biggest mistake at month twelve is splitting tasks across a spreadsheet, a notes app, and two email inboxes. Choose one planning home—whether that is WedCheese or another tool—and migrate everything there within the first fortnight. Future-you will not merge three versions of the guest list at month three.

12 months out: vision, budget, and the big three bookings

Month twelve is about foundations, not Pinterest rabbit holes. Get these in place before you fall in love with a dress you cannot afford or a venue that is already booked.

  • Set a realistic total budget and guest-count range—vendors will ask immediately.
  • Discuss priorities as a couple: venue vibe, photography, food, or live music—pick your non-negotiables.
  • Research and tour venues; peak UK Saturdays often book 12–18 months ahead in many regions.
  • Begin photographer and videographer shortlists—portfolios matter more than price alone.
  • Sketch a rough guest list A-list before save-the-dates commit you to numbers.
  • Open a shared planning workspace so both partners see the same timeline—not separate notes apps.

9–10 months out: lock core suppliers and creative direction

  • Sign venue contract and confirm catering path (in-house, approved list, or external).
  • Book photographer, officiant or registrar slot, and coordinator if hiring one.
  • Research bands, DJs, or ceremony musicians; hold dates with deposits where needed.
  • Start attire shopping—made-to-order gowns and suits often need six-plus months.
  • Define decor mood (minimal, garden party, black tie) so florists quote accurately later.
  • Begin vendor shortlists in your planning app with notes on each quote.

6–8 months out: guests, invitations, and florals

  • Send save-the-dates once venue and date are firm—digital is fine for informal weddings.
  • Book florist, cake maker, hair and makeup, and transport if needed.
  • Order invitations; allow time for proofs, printing, and international postage.
  • Set up digital RSVP links so guests can reply without downloading an app.
  • Plan rehearsal dinner or welcome drinks for out-of-town guests.
  • Start your decor checklist and colour palette—see our wedding colour palette guide for ideas.

3–5 months out: details, fittings, and RSVPs

  • Set RSVP deadline eight to ten weeks before the wedding; chase stragglers early.
  • Schedule dress and suit fittings; book final alterations windows.
  • Choose readings, music cues, and ceremony order with your officiant.
  • Order rings, draft personal vows, and buy wedding-party gifts.
  • Confirm rental items: linens, chairs, lighting, and signage.
  • Log deposits and balances in your budget tracker—our wedding deposit tracker guide explains what to record.

1–2 months out: confirmations—not redesigns

  • Finalise seating chart after RSVP cutoff; share headcount with catering.
  • Walk through day-of timeline with vendors and wedding party leads.
  • Apply for marriage licence or complete civil paperwork per local rules.
  • Pack emergency kit, favours, and guest book; label boxes by room or event.
  • Confirm final payments and gratuity plan—log everything in your budget.

Wedding week: execute, delegate, breathe

  • Hand vendors a one-page run sheet with contacts and arrival times.
  • Confirm dietary flags, vendor meals, and accessibility needs with venue.
  • Designate one trusted person for day-of questions so you stay present.
  • Charge phones, pack getting-ready bags, and sleep—seriously.

Month-by-month at a glance

If you prefer a quick reference, here is the rhythm: months 12–10 lock venue, budget, and core creative direction; months 9–7 book remaining suppliers and send save-the-dates; months 6–4 handle invitations, RSVPs, and fittings; months 3–1 confirm numbers, payments, and run sheets; wedding week is execution only. Skipping ahead feels productive until you book florals before the venue contract is signed—follow the sequence even when excitement pushes you forward.
