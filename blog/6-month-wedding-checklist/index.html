Planning a Wedding in 6 Months? The Accelerated Checklist You Need

Published June 2026~10 min read

You said yes. You picked a date six months out. Then you opened a 12-month wedding checklist and immediately felt behind. If that is you, breathe— a short engagement is absolutely doable. You just need a compressed wedding timeline that prioritises what cannot wait and skips the guilt of tasks that were meant for month ten.

This guide is your accelerated wedding checklist for roughly six months to go. Follow it week by week, and pair it with a planner that understands you started late—not one that keeps nagging you about tasks that should have happened a year ago.

Month 6–5: Lock the foundations (do this first)

  • Set your budget with a real contingency line—not an optimistic guess.
  • Book venue and catering immediately; popular dates disappear fast.
  • Confirm guest list draft so invites and per-head costs are realistic.
  • Book photographer and registrar/officiant before other "nice to have" vendors.
  • Start dress/suit shopping—alterations need time even on a tight schedule.

Month 4–3: Vendors, style, and invites

  • Florist, DJ or band, cake, hair and makeup—book in parallel, not sequentially.
  • Send save-the-dates digitally if post would cut it too close.
  • Order invitations; launch digital RSVP links as soon as the list is stable.
  • Plan honeymoon basics if travelling soon after the wedding.
  • Confirm ceremony details, readings, and any cultural events.

Month 2: Confirmations and logistics

  • Finalise menu tasting, bar package, and rental needs.
  • Build your wedding day timeline / run sheet with vendor arrival times.
  • Seating plan first draft; chase outstanding RSVPs aggressively.
  • Book transport, accommodation blocks, and rehearsal dinner.
  • Final dress fitting and suit pickup scheduled.

Month 1 and final week: Execute, do not reinvent

  • Confirm final headcount with caterer.
  • Pack emergency kit, payments for tips, and vendor contact sheet.
  • Walk through ceremony order and speeches with wedding party.
  • Delegate day-of tasks—you cannot be planner and bride/groom simultaneously.
